Russia–Ukraine: Power, Inequality, and Empire

A student website for the Global Issues final project

Thesis

Most people explain the Russia–Ukraine war with standard geopolitics—security, borders, alliances. That matters, but it’s not the whole story. Using Marxist and Postcolonial lenses, this project argues the crisis is also about energy dependency, global capitalism, imperial identity, and racialized responses. These deeper structures shape how the war started, how it’s fought, and how the world reacts.

Marxist & Postcolonial IR — Plain and Short

Marxist IR (in simple words)

  • Global politics runs through capitalism and class. States often back elites and big firms.
  • The world economy is a core–periphery system. Core countries gain more; others get locked into exporting raw stuff.
  • Crises (like wars) can boost profits for energy and arms companies, while regular people pay the price.

Postcolonial IR (in simple words)

  • Empires don’t vanish in people’s heads. Former empires keep a hierarchy mindset about neighbors.
  • Who gets sympathy and who gets ignored often follows racial/civilizational lines.
  • Power includes the power to define others’ identity and “truth.” Narratives matter.

Why these two? Together, they uncover money flows, class interests, imperial habits, and biased narratives that mainstream theories often skip.

How the structures show up in the Russia–Ukraine war

Energy dependency (core–periphery logic)

Before 2022, the EU got roughly 40% of its natural gas from Russia (IEA 2022). Russia needed the revenue; Europe needed the fuel. Ukraine sat between them with major pipelines. That’s a classic structure: a semi-periphery exporter (Russia) tied to core buyers (EU), with Ukraine in the middle. When war hit, energy prices spiked, energy firms posted record profits, and people faced higher bills.

Finance & constraints (IMF / World Bank)

For years, Ukraine relied on IMF/World Bank help with tough conditions (austerity, privatization). During the war, the World Bank said Ukraine’s economy “depends on external financing” (World Bank 2023). That shows how global finance can limit policy choices. Marxist IR says: this is not neutral—these systems can reproduce dependency.

Imperial identity & denial of sovereignty

Postcolonial IR helps explain why Russia talks about Ukraine as “not a real state” or “one people.” That’s the old empire mindset—claiming the power to define another nation’s identity. It’s about more than borders; it’s about status and history.

Racialized sympathy & refugee responses

Western support for Ukrainian refugees was fast and generous (good!), but the contrast with refugees from Syria or parts of Africa reveals a hierarchy in who is seen as “like us.” Postcolonial IR names that bias and shows how it shapes policy and media.

Digital platforms & narratives

The info war ran through private platforms (X/Twitter, TikTok, YouTube). Algorithms pushed viral frames; companies set the rules. That’s a mix of Marxist concerns (corporate control, profit from engagement) and postcolonial concerns (who gets to define “truth”).

Bottom line: These structures don’t replace geopolitics—but they change the map. They shape who hurts, who profits, and whose story wins.

Environmental Stress Mapping (Part I & II)

Part I — Stress & how it interacts with the crisis

  • Factors: energy transition shocks, heat/winter extremes, pollution from strikes, food system disruption (grain exports).
  • Type: mostly slow-moving (energy/food systems), with sudden spikes (infrastructure hits, floods from damage).
  • Effect: higher prices, blackouts, food insecurity → more instability, migration, and stress on local governance.

Part II — Political consequences

  • State authority: can weaken (service failures, displacement), but can also harden (emergency powers, centralization).
  • Cooperation or competition? both—grain corridors and aid deals show cooperation; energy cut-offs and blockades show exclusion.

What could actually help

Near-term harm reduction

  • Keep grain export corridors open; protect energy/water infrastructure.
  • Transparent refugee pathways (apply the same standard globally).
  • Targeted macro support to Ukraine with clear social protections (don’t make recovery = austerity only).

Longer-term structural fixes

  • Reduce EU fossil dependency (build storage, interconnectors, renewables, heat-pumps).
  • Debt/conditionality reforms so recovery doesn’t lock in dependency.
  • Media/algorithm transparency (label state media, audit recommender systems in conflicts).
  • Support local civil society (women’s groups, independent media, veterans’ rehab).

Diplomacy matters, but so do the systems: energy, finance, platforms, and norms. Fixing those lowers the odds of the next crisis.
