Russia–Ukraine Through Marxist & Postcolonial Lenses

Dylan Ryvkin • POLSC 276 • Fall 2025

People usually explain the war with traditional IR: Russia’s security and NATO vs. Ukraine’s sovereignty and deterrence. Those ideas matter, but they only cover the surface. Using Marxist IR and Postcolonial IR shows a deeper story: energy dependency and corporate profit, financial constraints through global institutions, and an imperial mindset that denies Ukraine’s full agency. These structures shape how the war started, how it’s told, and who gets empathy.

Marxist IR (short version)

Marxist IR says you can’t understand global politics without capitalism and class. States often protect the interests of powerful groups and investors, not just some neutral “national interest.” The world economy runs on a core–periphery pattern: richer “core” states capture more value from “semi-periphery” suppliers.

Russia is militarily strong but economically tied to exporting oil and gas to Europe. Crises can shift markets and boost profits for elites. As David Harvey argues, “crises are necessary for the reproduction of capitalism.” During this war, energy companies set profit records, and arms makers ramped up production while ordinary people paid higher bills.

Postcolonial IR (short version)

Postcolonial theory examines how old empires shape today’s politics. Russia’s rhetoric treats Ukraine as not fully sovereign—echoing Tsarist/Soviet ideas that Ukrainians and Russians are “one people.” Achille Mbembe describes imperial power as defining reality for others; that fits the Kremlin’s claims about Ukrainian identity.

The global response also revealed racial hierarchy. Ukrainian refugees received open borders and sympathy; refugees from the Middle East and Africa often face pushbacks and suspicion. Who is seen as “civilized” still affects policy and media attention.

Case Analysis

Energy Dependency & Capitalism

Before 2022, the EU got about 40% of its natural gas from Russia (IEA 2022). Russia relied on that revenue. Ukraine sits between them with key pipelines. That’s a structural tie, not a one-off choice. When war hit, prices spiked, oil and gas firms posted record earnings, and weapons contracts surged—classic Marxist dynamics of crisis benefiting capital.

Finance & Conditionality

For years, Ukraine depended on IMF programs and World Bank support that came with austerity and privatization. Even during the war, the World Bank wrote that Ukraine’s economy “depends on external financing” (World Bank 2023). That’s what Cox would call hegemony: material power plus institutions and ideas that make dependence feel “necessary.”

Imperial Logic & Global Sympathy

Putin’s claims that Ukraine is not a “real state” repeat an imperial script that denies Ukrainian agency. Meanwhile, the refugee response showed a racialized empathy gap—Ukrainians welcomed quickly while others are fenced out. Postcolonial theory explains both patterns.

Digital Platforms

The info war ran through private platforms (X/Twitter, TikTok, YouTube, Meta). Algorithms decide what gets seen; companies profit from engagement. That’s ideological power working through digital capitalism.

Environmental Stress (brief)

The war intersects with energy transition and food systems. Shocks to gas and grain markets drive inflation and strain governments, encouraging tighter controls at home and competition abroad. In this case, environmental stress tends to weaken state authority in poorer states and strengthen it in wealthier ones, while nudging some cooperation (LNG sharing, grain corridors) and also hardening rivalries.
